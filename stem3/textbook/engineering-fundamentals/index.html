The Engineer’s Blueprint: Design Process

The Engineering Design Cycle

Engineers rarely get things perfect the first time. Instead, they follow a cycle and keep improving their ideas:

  1. Define the problem — What issue are you solving? Who is affected?
  2. Identify & research — What are the design requirements and limits (budget, time, materials)?
  3. Brainstorm ideas — Generate different solutions, look at existing products for inspiration.
  4. Plan — Sketch designs, choose materials, and write step-by-step build instructions.
  5. Build a prototype — Create an MVP (Minimum Viable Product), the simplest working version.
  6. Test & improve — Try it out. What works? What fails?
  7. Feedback — Ask users what they like, what confuses them, and what they would change.
  8. Iterate — Refine the design based on testing and feedback.
  9. Communicate — Share the final design and its impact, and plan the next improvements.

This cycle never really ends — even successful products are updated and improved over time.

Real-world example: Dyson Vacuum

James Dyson, founder of Dyson, applied the engineering design cycle to redesign the vacuum cleaner:

  • Problem — Traditional vacuum bags clogged and lost suction.
  • Research — He studied industrial cyclone systems that spin dust out of the air using centrifugal force.
  • Brainstorm — He explored how to adapt cyclone technology for a home vacuum.
  • Prototyping & testing — He built thousands of prototypes, constantly testing and tweaking.
  • Communicate — The final bagless vacuum changed the market and inspired other companies.

Key message: even world‑changing products start as rough ideas and messy prototypes.

Materials Matter: Why We Don’t Build Bridges with Chocolate

Material properties

Materials are the substances that make up physical objects. They have properties that determine how useful they are in real designs.

  • Stress — How much internal force a material feels when external forces act on it. Stress helps engineers predict if something will deform or break.
  • Strain — How much a material actually changes shape (deforms) under stress; the ratio of change in length to original length.
  • Elasticity — The ability of a material to return to its original shape after being stretched or compressed.
  • Toughness — How much energy a material can absorb before fracturing.

Many materials follow Hooke’s law for small stretches: F = kx, where F is force, x is extension, and k is the spring constant (stiffness).

Engineers use stress, strain, elasticity, and toughness to choose safe, long‑lasting materials.

Why bridges use steel and concrete

Bridges must carry heavy loads for decades, often in harsh weather. That’s why engineers choose materials like steel and concrete instead of something weak or brittle, like chocolate.

  • Steel — High strength, good strain tolerance, and strong toughness. Ideal for beams, cables, and reinforcing bars.
  • Concrete — Very good at handling compressive stress and extremely tough when combined with steel reinforcement.

Together, steel and concrete create structures that can support traffic loads safely for many years.

Bernoulli’s Principle

Air, pressure, and lift

Air might look invisible, but it is a fluid with mass, density, and pressure. Bernoulli’s principle links the speed of a fluid to its pressure.

Bernoulli’s principle: when the speed of a fluid increases, its pressure (or potential energy) decreases, and vice versa.

This idea is crucial in aviation. Airplane wings and flaps are shaped and angled to control airflow and pressure, generating lift.

  • Flaps extend from the wing and tilt downwards during takeoff and landing.
  • They slow down airflow over certain parts of the wing and redirect air downwards.
  • Changes in speed and direction of the air change pressure, creating extra lift so the plane can leave the ground or land more safely.

Many technologies — from spray bottles to carburettors — also use Bernoulli’s principle to move fluids efficiently.
